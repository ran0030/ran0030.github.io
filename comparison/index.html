Regional vs Metro Comparison:
Patronage and Trends Analysis

from 2018 - 2024

SECTION 1:

Metro vs Regional Patronage Trends
Intro to the Data
This graph compares total yearly patronage between metropolitan and regional public transport services in Victoria from 2018 to 2024. Displaying both datasets together allows for direct comparison of overall scale, decline during COVID-19, and the pace of recovery in each network.
Analysis
Metropolitan transport consistently recorded significantly higher patronage than regional services throughout the entire period. In 2019, metropolitan patronage exceeded 567 million trips, compared to approximately 36 million regional trips, meaning metro usage was more than fifteen times larger.

Despite this difference in scale, both networks experienced sharp declines in 2020 due to COVID-19 restrictions. However, metropolitan patronage fell more dramatically, decreasing by 63%, while regional patronage declined by 51%.

From 2022 onwards, both systems showed recovery. However, regional patronage recovered slightly faster proportionally, eventually surpassing its pre-pandemic median levels by 2024, while metropolitan patronage still remains slightly below its 2019 peak.

SECTION 2:

Transport Share Distribution (2024)
Intro to the Data
This pie chart shows the proportion of total public transport patronage in Victoria during 2024, divided between metropolitan and regional services.

Analysis
In 2024, metro services account for approximately 92% of total rail passengers, while regional rail represents only a small proportion. This highlights the dominance of urban rail within the network and suggests that infrastructure investment and service planning must primarily support metropolitan demand.

However, despite Regional's smaller share, regional transport still plays an essential role in connecting regional communities and supporting long-distance commuting into Melbourne.

SECTION 3:

Mode Contribution Across Networks (2024)
Intro to the Data
This pie chart displays the percentage of the contribution of each transport mode to the total public transport network in 2024. It includes metropolitan trains, trams, buses, and regional services.

Analysis
Trains consistently contributed the largest share of total patronage across the entire period, highlighting their importance as the primary commuting mode within Melbourne. Trams also contributed a substantial portion, particularly due to their extensive network within the central city and inner suburbs.

Buses accounted for a smaller but still significant share, mainly supporting local and suburban connections.

Regional services contributed the smallest overall portion, reflecting both lower population density and longer travel distances outside metropolitan Melbourne.

SECTION 4:

Recovery Comparison
Intro to the Data
This graph compares yearly percentage changes in patronage for both metropolitan and regional networks. Analysing percentage change makes it easier to compare how strongly each network was affected by COVID-19 and how quickly they recovered.
Analysis
The largest decline occurred between 2019 and 2020, when metropolitan patronage fell by approximately 63%, compared to a 51% decline in regional services. This suggests metropolitan travel was more heavily affected by lockdowns and the shift to remote work.

Recovery began in 2022, when both networks experienced strong growth. Regional patronage increased by 48.5%, while metropolitan patronage rose by 56.6%.

Although both networks continued recovering through 2023 and 2024, growth rates slowed as patronage levels approached pre-pandemic values. By 2024, regional patronage had fully recovered and slightly exceeded its pre-pandemic median levels, while metropolitan patronage remained slightly below its 2019 peak.

SECTION 5:

Investigation Summary
Conclusion
Overall, the investigation shows that public transport patronage in Victoria experienced a significant disruption during the COVID-19 pandemic, particularly in 2020 and 2021. Both metropolitan and regional networks recorded substantial declines due to lockdowns, reduced commuting, and widespread remote work.

However, recovery patterns differed between the two networks. Metropolitan transport experienced a larger initial decline and has recovered more slowly, likely due to lasting changes in commuting behaviour such as hybrid work arrangements. In contrast, regional patronage declined less severely and recovered more quickly, eventually surpassing pre-pandemic median levels by 2024.

These findings suggest that metropolitan transport demand is more sensitive to changes in daily commuting patterns, while regional travel may be influenced by a broader range of purposes such as long-distance travel, tourism, and essential services.

Overall, the data indicates that while public transport usage in Victoria has largely recovered since the pandemic, long-term behavioural changes may continue to shape future demand, particularly within metropolitan Melbourne.

LIMITATIONS OF THE INVESTIGATION

While the dataset provides reliable patronage data across multiple years, the investigation does not account for population growth, service frequency changes, or fare policy adjustments that may also influence transport demand.
