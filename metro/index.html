Metropolitan Services:
Patronage and Trends Analysis

from 2018 - 2024

SECTION 1:

Overview of Metropolitan Transport
Intro to the Data
This section analyses total metropolitan public transport patronage in Victoria from 2018 to 2024. The data includes trains, trams and buses operating within metropolitan Melbourne. By looking at total yearly patronage, we can clearly see how usage patterns changed before, during and after COVID-19.
Analysis
From 2018 to 2019, metropolitan patronage remained relatively stable, reflecting consistent daily commuting across Melbourne. However, in 2020 there was a dramatic decline. Patronage fell by approximately 63.10% between 2019 and 2020, representing a decrease of over 357 million trips, the largest annual drop in the dataset. This sharp fall aligns with COVID-19 lockdowns, movement restrictions, and the rapid shift to remote work and online learning.

The steep decline highlights how heavily metropolitan transport depends on regular commuting patterns.

From 2022 onwards, usage began to recover as restrictions eased and people gradually returned to workplaces, schools and public spaces. Although there has been strong growth between 2022 and 2024, total patronage still remains slightly below pre-pandemic levels. This suggests that long-term behavioural shifts, such as hybrid work arrangements, may continue to influence public transport demand.

Ongoing infrastructure upgrades, such as the Metro Tunnel Project, Suburban Rail Loop, Regional Rail Revival and Level Crossing Removal Program, as well as service disruptions, may also have influenced short-term usage patterns in recent years.

SECTION 2:

Metro Mode Breakdown (Train vs Tram vs Bus)
Intro to the Data
This graph compares total yearly patronage for metropolitan trains, trams and buses from 2018 to 2024. Breaking the data down by mode makes it easier to see which type of transport was most affected and how each recovered over time.
Analysis
Before 2020, trains consistently recorded the highest patronage, followed by trams, while buses had the lowest total usage. This reflects Melbourne's transport structure, where trains serve as the primary mode for commuting from outer suburbs into the CBD.

During 2020 and 2021, all three modes experienced significant declines. However, trains showed the largest overall drop, suggesting they were most affected by lockdowns and the shift to remote work. As trains are heavily used for weekday commuting, reduced office attendance likely had a major impact.

Trams also experienced a substantial decline, particularly due to reduced activity within central Melbourne during lockdown periods.

By 2024, all three modes show clear recovery trends. However, none have fully returned to their pre-pandemic peak levels, indicating that commuting behaviour may have undergone lasting structural changes.

Monthly Trends and Seasonal Patterns
Intro to the Data
This section explores monthly metropolitan patronage trends for each individual year between 2018 and 2024. By breaking the data down into months instead of yearly totals, we can see more detailed patterns such as seasonal peaks, school holiday dips and the specific impact of lockdown periods.

Analysis
The monthly data reveals clear seasonal patterns in pre-pandemic years. Patronage typically peaks during school terms and declines during major holiday periods, particularly in January and during mid-year breaks.

In 2018 and 2019, this pattern is predictable and relatively stable, reflecting consistent commuting and school attendance across metropolitan Melbourne.

In contrast, 2020 and 2021 display highly irregular patterns. There are extreme declines during lockdown months, particularly during Melbourne's extended Stage 4 restrictions in mid-to-late 2020. These drops are significantly sharper than normal holiday-related declines, demonstrating the impact of strict movement restrictions.

From 2022 onwards, monthly usage becomes more stable again as recovery begins. By 2023 and 2024, seasonal trends reappear, although overall levels remain slightly below pre-pandemic peaks. This suggests that while daily routines have largely resumed, long-term changes such as hybrid work may still be influencing transport demand.

SECTION 4:

Variability & Distribution Analysis
Intro to the Data
This section looks at how consistent metropolitan transport usage was across each year. Instead of just focusing on totals, it analyses the spread and distribution of monthly data to see how stable travel patterns were before, during and after the pandemic, using a Box Plot chart.
Analysis
Metropolitan transport shows relatively consistent monthly variability in 2018 and 2019, with moderate spread between minimum and maximum values. However, in 2020 the distribution becomes far more dispersed. The box plot shows a significantly lower median and a wider interquartile range, reflecting unstable and unpredictable usage during lockdown periods.

In contrast, 2023 and 2024 show a tightening distribution compared to 2020-2021, suggesting that usage patterns are becoming more stable again, even if overall patronage has not fully returned to pre-pandemic levels.

Overall, the distribution analysis supports the earlier trend observations, confirming that 2020-2021 were not only lower in total patronage, but also significantly more unstable compared to pre- and post-pandemic years.
