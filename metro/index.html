Metropolitan Services:
Patronage and Trends Analysis

from 2018 - 2024

SECTION 1:

Overview of Metropolitan Transport
Intro to the Data
This section analyses total metropolitan public transport patronage in Victoria from 2018 to 2024. The data includes trains, trams and buses operating within metropolitan Melbourne. By looking at total yearly patronage, we can clearly see how usage patterns changed before, during and after COVID-19.
Analysis
From 2018 to 2019, metropolitan patronage was relatively stable, showing consistent high usage across Melbourne's transport network. However, in 2020 there was a dramatic decline due to COVID-19 restrictions, lockdowns and the shift to remote work and online learning.

The steepness of the decline between 2019 and 2020 highlights how dependent metropolitan transport is on daily commuting patterns.

From 2022 onwards, usage began to recover as restrictions eased and people returned to offices, schools and public spaces. While there has been strong growth between 2022 and 2024, total metropolitan patronage has not yet fully returned to pre-pandemic levels. This suggests that long-term behavioural changes, such as hybrid work and flexible schooling, may still be affecting public transport demand, as well as delays from onging transport projects, such as the Metro Tunnel Project, Suburban Rail Loop and Level Crossing Removal, which were the main causes for train delays in the past few years.

SECTION 2:

Metro Mode Breakdown (Train vs Tram vs Bus)
Intro to the Data
This graph compares total yearly patronage for metropolitan trains, trams and buses from 2018 to 2024. Breaking the data down by mode makes it easier to see which type of transport was most affected and how each recovered over time.
Analysis
Before 2020, trains clearly had the highest patronage out of the three modes, followed by trams, with buses having the lowest total usage. This makes sense because trains are the main way people commute into the CBD from outer suburbs, but could also hint an underestimation of bus patronage due to fare evading being a common occurence.

During 2020 and 2021, all three modes experienced a sharp decline. However, trains showed the largest drop overall, which suggests they were the most affected by lockdowns and remote work. Since trains are heavily used for daily office commuting, the shift to working from home likely reduced demand significantly.

Trams also experienced a major decline, particularly due to reduced activity in central Melbourne during lockdown periods.

By 2024, all three modes show clear recovery trends, but they all still remain below their pre-pandemic peak, indicating that commuting behaviour may have permanently shifted.
