Metropolitan Services:
Patronage and Trends Analysis

from 2018 - 2024

SECTION 1:

Overview of Metropolitan Transport
Intro to the Data
This section analyses total metropolitan public transport patronage in Victoria from 2018 to 2024. The data includes trains, trams and buses operating within metropolitan Melbourne. By looking at total yearly patronage, the change in usage patterns over time can be clearly understood, alongside the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic and related lockdowns.
Analysis
Between 2018 and 2019, metropolitan patronage remained relatively stable, reflecting consistent daily commuting across Melbourne. However, 2020 saw a dramatic decline in metropolitan transport usage. Patronage fell by approximately 63.10% between 2019 and 2020, which represented a decrease of over 357 million trips, the largest annual drop in the dataset. This sharp fall aligned with COVID-19 lockdowns, movement restrictions, and the rapid shift to remote work and online learning.

From 2022 onwards, usage began to recover as restrictions eased and people gradually returned to workplaces, schools and public spaces. Although there growth was strong between 2022 and 2024, total annual patronage failed to reach pre-pandemic levels. This suggests that long-term behavioural shifts, such as working-from-home arrangements, continue to influence public transport patronage.

Ongoing infrastructure upgrades, such as the Metro Tunnel Project, Suburban Rail Loop, Regional Rail Revival and Level Crossing Removal Program, and other service disruptions may also have influenced short-term usage patterns in recent years.

SECTION 2:

Metro Mode Breakdown (Train vs Tram vs Bus)
Intro to the Data
This graph compares total yearly patronage for metropolitan trains, trams and buses from 2018 to 2024. Breaking the data down by mode makes it easier to see which type of transport was most affected and how each recovered over time.
Analysis
Before 2020, trains consistently recorded the highest patronage, followed by trams, while buses had the lowest total usage. This reflects Melbourne's transport structure, where trains serve as the primary mode for commuter travel from outer suburbs into the CBD.

During 2020 and 2021, all three modes experienced significant declines. However, trains showed the largest overall drop, suggesting they were most affected by lockdowns and the shift to remote work. Reduced office attendance likely had a major impact on patronage due to the heavy usage of trains as weekday transport.

Trams also experienced a substantial decline, particularly due to reduced activity within the CBD and inner Melbourne suburbs during lockdown periods.

By 2024, all three modes showed clear recovery trends. However, none fully returned to their pre-pandemic peak levels, indicating that commuter behaviour may have undergone significant and lasting changes.

SECTION 3:

Monthly Trends and Seasonal Patterns
Intro to the Data
This section explores monthly metropolitan patronage trends for each individual year between 2018 and 2024. By breaking the data down into months instead of yearly totals, patterns such as seasonal peaks, school holiday dips and the specific impact of lockdown periods can be represented and easily comprehended.

Analysis
The monthly data revealed clear seasonal patterns in pre-pandemic years. Patronage typically peaked during school terms and declined during major holiday periods, particularly in January and during mid-year breaks.

In 2018 and 2019, patronage patterns were predictable and relatively stable, which reflected consistent commuter travel across metropolitan Melbourne.

In contrast, 2020 and 2021 displayed highly irregular patterns. There were extreme declines during lockdown months, particularly during Melbourne's extended Stage 4 restrictions in mid-to-late 2020. Those drops were significantly sharper than normal holiday-related declines, demonstrating the impact of the strict travel regulations imposed by the Victorian Government in response to the pandemic threat.

2022 saw monthly usage became more stable again as recovery began. By 2023 and 2024, standard seasonal trends reappeared, although overall patronage remained slightly under pre-pandemic averages. This suggests that while daily routines largely resumed, long-term changes such as hybrid work may have continued to influence transport demand.

SECTION 4:

Variability & Distribution Analysis
Intro to the Data
This section contains an analysis of the consistency of metropolitan transport usage across each year. Instead of a strict focus on totals, it analyses the spread and distribution of monthly data to understand the stablility of travel patterns before, during and after the pandemic, using a Box Plot chart.
Analysis
Metropolitan transport showed relatively consistent monthly variability in 2018 and 2019, with moderate spread between minimum and maximum values. However, in 2020 the distribution became far more dispersed. The box plot displayed a significantly lower median and a wider interquartile range, reflecting unstable and unpredictable usage during lockdown periods.

In contrast, 2023 and 2024 presented a tightening distribution compared to 2020-2021, suggesting that usage patterns were returning to a stable state, despite overall patronage not quite recovering to pre-pandemic levels.

Overall, the distribution analysis supported the earlier trend observations, confirming the both the significant decline in and relative instability of patronage during 2020 and 2021, when compared to pre- and post pandemic data.
